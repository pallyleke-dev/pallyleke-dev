The Brief

2D technical drawings are the universal language of manufacturing. Every part that gets machined, cast, or fabricated starts as a flat drawing — a precise document of dimensions, tolerances, materials, and assembly instructions. The challenge was to annotate these drawings so that AI models could read them the way an experienced engineer does.

The source files came in AutoCAD DWG, VectorWorks VWX, and LibreCAD DXF formats — a variety that required fluency across platforms. Each drawing had dozens of dimension lines, tolerance callouts, part title blocks, and revision markers that all needed to be tagged, classified, and structured for machine learning ingestion.

The Process

Step 01
Drawing Intake
Each drawing was opened and reviewed — understanding the part geometry, reading the title block, and identifying all dimension and annotation elements before labelling began.
Step 02
Title Block Extraction
Part name, number, material, revision, and drawing scale were extracted from the title block and recorded as structured metadata fields for each drawing.
Step 03
Dimension Annotation
Every dimension line was tagged with its value, unit, and type — linear, radial, angular, or ordinate. Dimension chains were traced to ensure no measurement was missed.
Step 04
Tolerance Tagging
Geometric tolerances (GD&T callouts), surface finish symbols, and fit tolerances were identified and labelled with their type and value — critical for AI understanding of precision requirements.
Step 05
Part Feature Labelling
Key geometric features — holes, slots, chamfers, fillets, threads — were labelled by type and linked to their dimensional annotations to create a complete part feature map.
Step 06
QA & Export
Each annotated drawing was reviewed against the original to confirm nothing was missed, then exported in the required structured format with a full annotation manifest.

Challenges & Solutions

Inconsistent drawing standards. Drawings arrived in a mix of ISO, ANSI, and company-specific standards — each with different tolerance notation, symbol sets, and dimension placement conventions. A cross-reference guide was built to normalise labels regardless of source standard.

Overlapping annotations. In densely detailed drawings, dimension lines often overlapped or shared endpoints. Each annotation had to be traced to its parent feature individually — no assumptions about what a line belonged to.

Legacy DXF files. Older LibreCAD DXF files occasionally had broken geometry or missing layers. These were repaired in LibreCAD before annotation proceeded.
