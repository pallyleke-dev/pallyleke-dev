The Context

DHL's Manchester operation ran around the clock — multiple shifts, thousands of parcels, dozens of data touchpoints per hour. In that kind of environment, bad data doesn't just look bad on a report. It causes missed collections, incorrect routing, client complaints, and real financial cost.

My role as an Associate placed me at the heart of that data flow. I wasn't just processing — I was responsible for generating the reports that shift managers and operations leads relied on to make decisions. Every number I put on a dashboard had to be right.

The Process

Step 01
Data Source Connection
Connected IBM Cognos Analytics to the operational database, mapping data sources for parcel throughput, scan events, error logs, and shift productivity metrics.
Step 02
Report Framework Design
Designed a standardised report layout covering: daily throughput by shift, data accuracy rate, error type breakdown, and workflow bottleneck indicators.
Step 03
Data Validation Layer
Built validation checks into the reporting pipeline — flagging anomalous scan counts, duplicate entries, and timestamp mismatches before data reached the dashboard.
Step 04
Daily Report Generation
Reports were generated at shift end and distributed to operations leads. Each report included a narrative summary highlighting any flagged anomalies or performance trends.
Step 05
Weekly Trend Analysis
Weekly reports aggregated daily data to surface patterns — recurring error types, peak throughput periods, and shifts consistently below accuracy targets.
Step 06
Process Improvement Input
Reporting findings were fed into weekly ops review meetings. Data-backed observations led to process changes that measurably reduced recurring error types.

Dashboard Visual

Challenges & Solutions

Data latency. Scan data from the warehouse floor sometimes arrived late or out of sequence. The reporting pipeline had to accommodate this — holding final numbers until a cut-off time rather than reporting prematurely.

Anomaly patterns. Certain error types appeared repeatedly at the same time of day or from the same workstation. Identifying these patterns required looking beyond single-shift data to multi-week trends — something the weekly reports were specifically designed to surface.

Report adoption. Early versions of the dashboard were too detailed for shift managers to digest quickly. The design was simplified — key metrics front and centre, detailed breakdowns behind a second tab — leading to much higher uptake in daily briefings.
