The Brief

The task was straightforward on paper but demanding in practice: take a set of complex 3D mechanical assemblies — the kind that go inside industrial machines, conveyor systems, and manufacturing equipment — and annotate every single component with precision labels that an AI model could learn from.

These weren't simple shapes. The assemblies included interlocking shafts, helical gears, bearing housings, fasteners, and multi-part joints — components that looked similar from certain angles but were functionally completely different. The AI needed to learn those differences. My job was to teach it.

The Process

Step 01
Model Intake & Review
Each assembly was opened in FreeCAD and reviewed in full — rotating the model, inspecting the part tree, and understanding how components related to each other before touching a single label.
Step 02
Label Schema Design
A consistent label taxonomy was established: SHAFT_xx for rotational elements, GEAR_xx for transmission parts, JOINT_xx for connection points, and ASSEMBLY_vx for version tracking.
Step 03
Component-Level Annotation
Each part was selected individually within FreeCAD's model tree and assigned its label. Material properties, dimensional constraints, and spatial relationships were recorded alongside each tag.
Step 04
Edge Case Handling
Parts that overlapped spatially or shared attachment points required special handling — sub-labels were created to distinguish parent and child components without breaking the annotation schema.
Step 05
Quality Review
Every annotated assembly was reviewed against the original specification. Mislabelled parts, missing tags, and inconsistent naming were corrected before export.
Step 06
Export & Handoff
Final annotated models were exported in structured formats compatible with AI training pipelines, with a accompanying label manifest for each assembly version.

Challenges & How I Solved Them

Overlapping geometries. Several assemblies had components that physically overlapped in the 3D space — a shaft running through a gear housing, for example. Standard single-label approaches broke down here. I solved this by introducing a parent-child sub-labelling convention that kept the hierarchy clear without conflating separate parts.

Inconsistent source files. Some imported models had incomplete part trees or merged geometry — common with STEP and IGES imports. I had to manually split merged bodies in FreeCAD's Part workbench before annotation could begin, adding time but ensuring label accuracy.

Version control. With multiple assembly versions, keeping annotations consistent across iterations was critical. I maintained a label manifest per version and cross-referenced before each export to catch any schema drift.
