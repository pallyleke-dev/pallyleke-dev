The Context

RGIS is one of the world's largest inventory counting companies — clients include major retailers, warehouses, and distribution centres. When RGIS sends a team to count your stock, the numbers they come back with have to be exact. There is no margin for error because those numbers feed directly into purchasing decisions, financial reports, and loss prevention systems.

As an Inventory Specialist, I was deployed to client sites across Manchester — retail stores, stockrooms, and warehouses — to conduct physical stock counts. My job wasn't just to count accurately; it was to identify where the numbers didn't add up and produce reports that explained why.

The Process

Step 01
Pre-Audit Preparation
Before each deployment, expected stock counts were downloaded from the client's system. Audit zones were mapped and counting assignments distributed to ensure full site coverage.
Step 02
Physical Count Execution
Stock was counted systematically — aisle by aisle, shelf by shelf — using RGIS scanning equipment. Every SKU was scanned and actual quantities logged against the expected baseline.
Step 03
Variance Detection
On completion, actual counts were compared against expected values. SKUs with variance beyond tolerance thresholds were automatically flagged for secondary review.
Step 04
Root Cause Analysis
Flagged discrepancies were investigated on-site — checking for miscounts, misplaced stock, damaged items, or system data errors — and the root cause documented for each.
Step 05
Discrepancy Report
A structured discrepancy report was produced for each client — listing flagged SKUs, variance quantities, root cause findings, and recommended corrective actions.
Step 06
Data Integrity Sign-off
Final counts were validated, reports signed off, and data submitted to the client system. Any post-submission queries were resolved with full audit trail documentation.

What Made This Work

Systematic counting discipline. The temptation in high-volume environments is to move fast. But speed without accuracy is worthless in audit work. I developed a zone-by-zone counting discipline that prioritised completeness over pace — ensuring nothing was skipped, even in cluttered or poorly organised stockrooms.

Root cause thinking. A variance is just a number until you explain it. I treated every flagged discrepancy as a puzzle — was it a miscount, a system error, misplaced stock, or genuine loss? Each had a different resolution path and the report needed to reflect that distinction clearly.

Client communication. Discrepancy reports went directly to clients. The language had to be clear, the findings unambiguous, and the recommendations actionable. This was professional documentation under real commercial stakes.
